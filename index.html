It was a much bigger project than I anticipated, which is what I attribute to such things being ignored by directors and managers as ‘too hard’ and too easy to ignore. No matter how far I go in life I will always remember this: The ground staff know more than their managers and the talent to fix these issues is in them as well.

My ideal job would be in the medical profession as a cross trained clinical and IT member to coordinate and implement these health care systems locally and across networks. I am a problem solver and I am also a problem finder. I find errors in systems and correct them. The reason these errors are not recognised is that they have been built incorrectly due to the disconnect between the high level professional capabilities of clinical and IT staff. Coming from a clinical background I intend to bridge that gap to have a greater impact on improving health care than I am capable of now. I have spent the last few years attaining an MBA and an MSc in health care management to understand the managerial state of mind. The next step is to improve my IT capabilities and credentials to be able better articulate and hopefully soon, to make the changes others are not equipped to make.



Overview:


This proposal is to have a check program that can be shared between IT and clinical personnel for the development of a new system, or as a routine check for existing medical data networks. It is simple to run and offers side by side comparisons for simple presentation to the user.

This will eliminate confusing mixes of terms that given the sheer number of cases do lead to adverse medical outcomes.


Motivation:


My goal, starting with this first proper step into IT, is to improve patient care and outcomes in the digital space within which our hospitals and clinics operate. Using the hospital as a familiar example, this can be just as easily implemented into any large or small scale clinic or organisation. Each hospital has an EMR (Electronic Health Record) which is the primary source of a patient’s information. This EMR is typically connected to around 200 others systems and middle-wares. An EMR is connected to pharmacy, laboratory and even dietaries for meal restrictions:


Figure 1: https://cloudsmallbusinessservice.com/small-business/best-emr-systems-software-electronic-medical-record-systems.html



A confusing factor that emerges from all of this integration is that similar, but different names or terms are used for the same description in different systems. For example, ‘lung washing’ may be collected in theatre, but appear on the laboratory system as ‘bronchial lavage’. This isn’t inherently incorrect, but is a difference great enough that causes confusion in such a highly specific industry. A greater example may be ‘lung fluid’, which frankly could be any number of things vs the specific ‘bronchial lavage’ which should be the same across systems and disciplines. A ‘lung fluid’ could be ordered, but interpreted by the lab as a cyst drainage getting well off track from the intention.
Ultimately it is the patient that suffers from mistreatment and clinical personnel in time spent collaborating.

These errors can easily be mitigated by a check program that compares the same outputs of each of the systems. This would save an organisation or network great amounts of time checking and conferring between departments, as well as mitigate the risk for misinterpretation and patient treatment.

Description:


The following is an example of the project that inspired me to learn IT on a path towards health care informatics:

Figure 2: Smith, S. 2017. Microbiology Container and Specimen Types. Presentation to PLMI UBC, 5th of October 2017 at CCAD.



Figure two shows two examples of common terms that are completely valid within their own systems (hospital system and lab system), but have completely different relevancies in practice. The terms shown are inadequate and the growth of these bacteria are normal and irrelevant in the laboratory for most clinical circumstances.

“Wound culture” is too ambiguous, but is commonly used. The pictures for wound culture show a normal skin flora on the left that is clinically insignificant and not reported. The same shown on the right would be the cause of serious infection that needs to be treated.

“Fluid” from the body can contain any number of normal flora (normal bacteria), however some fluids are from inner and sterile parts of the body where the same normal bacteria can cause harm and infection.

This is but one, very small, example of a clinical situation where specificity is absolutely key. The health care system is immensely greater than this scenario and one can imagine the amount of time a physiotherapist must put into contacting doctors, wards and surgeons to determine the right recovery process for a patient without the proper clinical picture that should be a standardised language.



Tools and technologies:



There is a tool, or tools rather, that are already implemented in health care, but are not strictly mandatory. These are the SNOWMED (for semantics in language) nomenclature system and HL7 (Health Language version 7). Implementation of these systems is common, but what they don’t do is translate the information to the end user.

For example: SNOWMED may dictate ECOL as the code for E. coli across systems, but the text presented may be E. coli, Escherichia coli, EC, or Enterobacteriaciae. This may confuse an end user who is used to seeing a particular terminology as they quickly read or review a record.

A better example would be Lung Fluid which may be LFLD under SNOWMED and each system’s database, but will expand to ‘Lung Fluid’ in the pathology lab, ‘Fluid, Lung’ in the operating theatre, ‘Body Fluid, Lung’ in pharmacy and ‘Body Fluid’ in the rehabilitation ward. The meaning changes slightly each time and the correspondence between departments becomes confusing. This needs to be standardised across all network platforms.

The new tool to do this is a software program that can receive manual input from each department as to what they would like as a terminology during the development stage so a democratic choice can be made. Alternatively, a standardised input for all departments can be created and kept on the one database.

Figure 3: Smith, S. 2021. Assignment 1 for Introduction to IT. 20/01/21.


Figure 3 shows the simple comparison that can be made. With the background SNOWMED code on the left corresponding to its expanded text in each system. ‘Penicillin’ has been shortened to ‘Pen.’ In the emergency department. This discrepancy is easily recognised after scanning and highlighted for possible correction.



Skills required:


The skill sets to run such a program are more than present in a health care environment. The difficulty being the communication between the IT professionals and clinical professionals that speak different specialised languages. For example, it cannot be expected for an IT professional to have the clinical education as well to distinguish between subtly different types of surgical specimens. Neither is it reasonable to expect a surgeon to be able to comprehend the informatics and information processing between the OR and pharmacy.

Thus, a program that can detect and highlight language deficiencies amongst the great number of systems is a hugely helpful tool. Once a detection is made a side by side comparison can be made. A clinical person can verify the correct terminology and the system administrator can make the correction.

Outcome:


The dictionary of terms and nomenclature is absolutely key to clear and efficient communication. Contributions to the shared master document can allow for direct comparisons of terms across all medical disciplines so that interpretation is consistent and clear. This master document can be given access to automatically update terms in each system once a consensus is achieved, or can withhold a report from release if there is mismatch in clinical terminology.

It is well known that requests for change are often submitted to IT from clinicians, so this ‘gate keeper’ can alert a non-clinical IT professional of a possible conflict in other areas of the network. The program can run live in the background in case of a change, or can periodically scan health care system for inconsistencies that can presented to a user with a side by side comparison that is easy to resolve.

Project Idea (Dylan Kassiotis)


Overview

My project idea is a study aid application named Moleti (Greek word for study). Moleti will help students with memorization by having a flashcard and Pomodoro timer functionality. Secondly, Moleti will also have notetaking that would allow the user to reflect upon their notes in the Cornell notetaking method. And Moleti will have organizational benefits by allowing the user to make their study calendar to evolve around their life schedule. As a result of these functionalities students should be able to memorise the information for longer and be able to reflect upon there notes at ease after there studies.


Motivation

I believe many of the features of Moleti will have a beneficial impact on decision making, productivity and memorisation of its users. For instance, (Danziger, Levav and Avnaim-Pesso, 2011) reveal in a study that during daily proceedings, the likelihood of a favourable conviction from a judge will gradually fall around 65%. However, after the judge has had recess the likelihood of a favourable and fair conviction will rise by around 65%. Secondly, (Ariga and Lleras, 2011) did a study that uncovers that the decrementation of focus during long task’s such as studying is avoidable by implementing brief breaks where you focus your attention on a secondary task.


Description

Moleti will revolutionize the way you study, available on desktop and online, it will have the feature Pomodoro Method with this feature you will be able to use the popular time management technique developed by Francesco Cirillo.

To use this feature, you can click the task section to write down your objectives to complete during the Pomodoro. Meanwhile, set the estimated time to complete that task (note one Pomodoro equals twenty-five minutes). Afterwards, select the task that you would like to complete then start the timer. Once started, work for twenty-five minutes then have a five-minute break when the audio notification system starts. If you do not want to do the pre-set timer you can also customize the time to suit your preference.

The second feature Moleti has is the Cornell note-taking system, with this feature you can create a new document and then write in the Cornell style, edit it or change its layout, delete it, download as a pdf, or print and share it. Additionally, you can click the flashiphy button to make the bullet points into flashcards. With the third feature of flashcards, you can download pre-existing online or make your own. The Flashcards are also downloadable to print or share. This is a useful tool for revision and memorization.

Lastly, the Organizer feature is an organization tool that can be used to make a mind map, a to-do list, or an org-chart, this feature is ideal for teamwork as you can set objectives and see what your team mates are doing.


Tools and techologies

The Tools and technologies required in order to make a web and software application would be programming languages such as

  • HTML which handles the text
  • CSS is the colour and design elements
  • lastly JavaScript which is used for the animations For the ide Atom would be used as a text editor, file manager, compiler and a translator.
Adobe Illustrator or Photoshop would be used for the design of the user interface components. Another language that could be used is Java which is an object-oriented programming language that supports web and desktop (Windows) applications, it is also compatible for Android applications.


Skills Required

To make the application, experience with the programming languages: Java, CSS, HTML, and JavaScript would be needed. Furthermore, knowledge of the IDE Atom would be essential to be able to use the languages listed. Additionally, the general ability to be able to find (google) the solution to a roadblock. In the case of not having these skills, it is quite easy to find the skills and software simply Google Atom or the programming languages. However, to learn them, I would recommend online recourses such as YouTube, Udemy, or W3Schools.


Outcome

In the case of the project being successful, it would have immensely improved the memorisation and organization for students around the world. By, providing its users with the Pomodoro method to aid the effectiveness of their studying. Additionally, the implementation of the Cornell Note-taking system which would aid with more organised notes and the reduction of editing. Consequently, The application could improve the average passing rate of students and their ability to retain the information.

Phone interview conducted with Raymond Buchannan – Senior Manager of LIS (Laboratory Information Systems) at Cleveland Clinic Abu Dhabi. Conducted at 0200 24/01/21.



What kind of work is done by the IT professional?



Well, the IT professional generally speaking is a very all-round talent. There are many specialised courses, which are great, but there is everything from coding base programs to customer support.

Communication skills are essential as there is a middle-ground between the client and the IT professional. We can’t be talking IT to someone in clinical services, but we need to understand what they need in a very clinical setting.

Flexibility is also very important. We spend a lot of time trouble shooting with our clients, so it’s important to know as much about their role as we can. Being able to operate the systems is one thing, but to deliver on client expectations by developing the systems with the client are the most crucial.

What kinds of people does the IT professional interact with? Are they other IT professionals? Clients? Investors? The general public?

The IT professional interacts with the whole diversity of people. Right now at Cleveland Clinic these are mostly clinical professionals who need a problem solved. It is usually a training issue that we can walk our client through working together.

Perhaps the most wonderful thing about working in IT is that there are no borders to the internet and we have professionals from a global resource pool to work with instead of more local talent. It is always so fantastic to count the number of countries represented.

The IT professional will interact with all of the above depending on their role. There is an important distinction to be made between IT and computer science. Some people are gifted in their ability to create from a computer science background, whereas the IT professional requires an all-round talent to be able to communicate with a very diverse clientele.



Where does the IT professional spend most of their time?

As it is no surprise, I’m sure, we spend most of our time on our laptops. The second most important piece of equipment is the phone.

During the lead up to operations beginning at CCAD we had a very small team of three people. We began this project working from an office building and transferring and building the data base. This was a 9 hour a day operation for us to have the system ready before the clinical staff arrived.

When clinical staff began arriving we moved into the hospital to be on site. Our day was still very much the same working through issues identified and training clinical staff in the use of the EMR and LIS [electronic medical record, laboratory information system].

Today, we spend our time mostly at our desks with a team of 6 people mostly troubleshooting, but also creating opportunities for improvement. We are watching live feeds of information being passed between the LIS and EMR and catching data errors at the gates. We will correct these errors so that the data may flow through and not impact our clinical operators. We spend a lot of time troubleshooting with our clients on the phone, mostly helping them to perform tests orders and navigate their systems.

What aspect of their position is the most challenging?


The most wonderful part of the role and the most challenging is the cultural diversity. It is great to trouble shoot with people of so many different backgrounds, but it is crucial to remember that cultural differences and English being a second or fourth language can lead to mistranslation or slight understanding. I’ve worked in the US most of my life and that is certainly a difference between then and working in the Middle East. It is an absolute joy, but communication is always the most important and most challenging wherever we are in the world.

I would also have to mention that IT staff are never truly off work. We always have someone on call at least for clinical support, but most nights we are still working from home. Many times into the late hours of the night. It is a pleasure and a privilege, but also comes at the cost of managing one’s personal commitments.
